A tight lower bound cannot be raised. Suppose f = omega(n^2). Then I know the growth rate of f is strictly greater than the growth rate of n^2. Maybe it's log n * n^2. If it was, then a tight lower bound would be f = Omega(log n * n^2). I can do this for whatever the growth rate of f is. Whatever it is, that growth rate is a better bound than n^2.

As for theta, the fact that it is also an upper bound does not mean it is not a lower bound. If I give you f = theta(n^2) and I ask what is a tight lower bound for f, you should answer "n^2". You should not answer "I can't tell you because n^2 is also a tight upper bound".

Theta is both a tight lower bound and a tight upper bound.

Omega *may* be a tight lower bound or it may not be. It is a tight lower bound in this case: mergesort = Omega(n log n). It is not in this case: mergesort = Omega(1). Both statements are true, but only the first is a tight bount.

omega can never be a tight bound. It might be the best *known* bound, however.

Similar of Omicron and omicron.
